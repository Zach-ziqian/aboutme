About Me

Introduction

I am a PhD student in the History Department at the University of Washington, with research interests in modern Chinese history, legal history, and the history of gender and sexuality. My work explores the complex intersections of law, society, and identity in twentieth-century China.

I focus on crime in the Mao era, a time without clear legal codes. How people managed to live and how the state disciplined them are questions that fascinate me. Using so-called rubbish materials (垃圾材料), I explore the lives of ordinary people in villages and factories. My current project is entitled as "Legacy in Conflict: Legal Transformations and the Age of Sexual Consent in Modern China."

I have presented my work at several academic venues, including the Young Scholars Conference at UC San Diego and workshops on local history in Zhejiang. Through archival research and critical analysis, I aim to contribute to our understanding of how legal frameworks shaped personal experiences and social relationships in modern Chinese society.

When I'm not immersed in historical research, I love exploring Seattle in my spare time, capturing the city's vibrant neighborhoods and scenic landscapes through photography. I have a passion for collecting mechanical pencils and fountain pens, appreciating both their craftsmanship and the tactile experience of writing with quality instruments.
